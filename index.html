A little about me: I work as a project assistant under the supervision of Prof. Chandra R. Murthy in the Electrical Communication Engineering department at the Indian Institute of Science. I graduated from BITS Pilani, India in 2020 with a BE degree in electrical and electronics engineering. My bachelor's thesis was supervised by Prof. Chandra.

Research summary: My research centers around user mobility and its impact on the performance of multiple-antenna systems. Temporal variations in the channel response resulting from user movement cause a disparity between the estimated channel and the true channel. This is known as channel aging. I aim to characterise the consequent drop in the achievable performance measure (per-user rate or sum rate) as a function of mobility.

With my collaborators, I have explored the above problem in various settings thus far. These include:

  • Cell-free massive MIMO: Cell-free massive MIMO is a distributed variant of the cellular massive MIMO architecture offering higher coverage probability to users at the cost of increased backhaul. Currently, I am investigating the impact of channel aging and pilot contamination on the downlink performance of cell-free massive MIMO systems. This is a joint work with Prof. Ribhu Chopra at IIT Guwahati and Prof. Chandra at IISc.

  • OFDM-based massive MIMO: In OFDM systems, the Doppler effect resulting from user mobility brings an additional impairment known as inter subcarrier interference (ICI). In my bachelor's thesis, I looked at the joint effect of subcarrier allocation and user mobility on the uplink performance of multi-user massive MIMO-OFDM systems, for two receive combining schemes: maximum-ratio (MR) and zero-forcing (ZF). We got a bunch of interesting results, and submitted a paper in IEEE TCOM (under review).

I use tools from statistical signal processing, wireless communications and (a little bit of) information theory in my work.
